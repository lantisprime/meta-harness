# Resistance-as-signal — your scorn at new abstractions is data about you

Schillings narrates his own 45-year pattern of being wrong the same way each
time a layer of control was taken from him:

- Assembly years → "you look at **compilers** with a lot of suspicion — are
  those things really working correctly?"
- C++ years → "you look at **garbage collected languages** as *that's not
  real programming, you need to manage your memory*."
- 2018 → "some people were talking about vibe coding, writing code in
  English, and at the time honestly **I totally dismissed that** — English is
  not a programming language. Well, I guess I was pretty wrong."
- Today → "I use Python and vibe coding. Even old dogs can learn new tricks."

The meta-skill is not open-mindedness in general; it is **pattern-matching
your own resistance**. The feeling of "that's not real engineering" is
itself a datum with a strong historical base rate: it fires precisely when an
abstraction threatens hard-won mastery, and it has been wrong at every layer
transition. The expert response is to notice the emotion, recall the
analogue ("this is my compiler-suspicion again"), and convert the dismissal
into a test: use the new layer on real work before ruling on it. He also
generalizes it outward: the organizational version ("in 2018 nobody at
Google would give us the time of day — why would you ever need ML to write
code?") means institutional dismissal of a capability is equally weak
evidence against it.
