# The gold we cannot see — audit the bias in what "looks right"

"Humans are **incredibly biased in what we feel is the correct solution**.
We're the result of an evolutionary training that helped us survive in the
jungle — not doing quantum computing. Even though we can be brilliant and
innovative, there are whole ranges of progress and breakthroughs which we
just **cannot see or perceive**."

The epistemic skill: treat your sense of solution-plausibility as a trained
prior with a known training distribution — and therefore known blind spots.
The feeling that an approach is "natural" or "elegant" is evidence about
your training, only weakly about the solution space.

Two operational consequences he draws:

- **Value alien viewpoints instrumentally.** "ML is such a different
  viewpoint on many of those problems that we're going to get the *oh my
  god, this was in front of us the whole time and we could not see it*."
  A collaborator with a differently-biased prior (machine or human) is a
  search strategy, not a convenience.
- **Expect breakthroughs in plain sight.** The prediction isn't that hidden
  things will be found far away, but that they sit in already-explored
  territory, filtered out by everyone's shared prior. Nature "did an
  incredible engineering job and a terrible job at documentation" — the
  information exists; the bottleneck is a reader without our blinders.
