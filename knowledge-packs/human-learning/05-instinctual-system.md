# The Instinctual System — predictive relationships

The Instinctual System selects actions by **predicting when to release innate, genetically-programmed actions** (not learned). It only stores **simple predictive relationships** — which cue in which context predicts a survival-relevant state: food, danger, safety, and (in humans especially) **social belonging**. It learns via repeated recognition of predictors (Pavlovian/Rescorla-style prediction).

Why educators care about it most for the social/emotional dimension:

- It **controls emotional, physiological, and mental arousal**, and *all cognitive processes are affected by its continual action* — one thinks differently when angry, scared, comfortable, or complacent.
- When a student feels unsafe, it is because they perceive a combination of cues that predicted danger or lack of belonging in the past. To learn new cues, those danger predictions must be **confounded with salient experiences of safety and respect in the moment**. The system can then learn that cues predicting rejection in some environments do not predict it in this one.
- Actions include external (running from a threat) and internal (feeling angry at rejection).

**Teaching implications.** Learners *use* this system whenever they decide whether it's safe to ask questions or make mistakes; they *train* it whenever they work outside their comfort zone — outcomes there raise or lower expectations of safety/belonging in that context. Because the system learns from any relevant predictive cue present, **instinctual learning is inherently integrated into all other learning**: if a procedural or deliberative exercise exposes students to disdain for asking questions, the Instinctual System learns those threat predictors regardless. So it must be **intentionally designed into every exercise** and can also be trained in isolation (icebreakers, community-building). The corresponding feedback is **"who"** — direct attention to the human element and the students' own somatic states, and frame the classroom as a safe space for learning risks. A concrete move: **reframe frustration and confusion as an ordinary part of learning rather than a predictor of failure**, and directly tell students they belong and are expected to succeed.
