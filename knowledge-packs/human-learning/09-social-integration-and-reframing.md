# Social integration and reframing challenge as progress

Social integration — full participation in meaningful relationships with peers and instructors — is one of the strongest enablers of persistence. The model's claim is that it is **not governed by the Instinctual System alone** but arises from **all four systems working together**:

- **Procedural**: performs routine help-seeking behaviors (asking questions, attending office hours) as effortless habits, so students don't spend limited energy deliberating over them.
- **Deliberative**: lets students explicitly form expectations of being treated with respect in new situations.
- **Perceptual**: categorizes challenging or unpleasant experiences — which might signal rejection elsewhere — as indicators of acceptance and progress *in this context*.
- **Instinctual**: produces the calm, attentive somatic state and the social bonding.

Three common strategies, sharpened by the model:

1. **Minimize extraneous difficulty** (while preserving desirable difficulty). Parsing exercises into the four types makes this simpler: anticipate the *delayed payoff* of perceptual learning, the *vulnerability* of instinctual learning, the *confusion/frustration* of deliberative learning, and the *fatigue/boredom* of procedural learning, and mitigate each specifically.
2. **Set norms collaboratively** (Deliberative × Procedural). Make genuinely *routine* social actions (raising a hand, attending a study session) required early and often so the Procedural System automates them — forcing students to *deliberate* over routine help-seeking disadvantages some (e.g. first-generation students facing the hidden curriculum). For *novel* social choices, give options, reasons, and clear, consistent consequences so the Deliberative System can plan.
3. **Reframe challenge** (Perceptual × Instinctual). The strongest lever: when the Instinctual System responds to danger predictors but outcomes are **repeatedly safer than expected in that situation**, the Perceptual System forms a new category *and* the Instinctual System learns new safety predictors — so students genuinely *experience* struggle as progress. This works best when guidance (a) helps students attend to what makes this situation different, (b) pairs the experience with clear messages of acceptance and progress, (c) points them to their internal somatic state as the predictor, and (d) is delivered **in the moment the struggle occurs**. Supportive instructors and peers can thereby become part of the student's identity group.
