# The Deliberative System — causal and structural relationships

The Deliberative System selects actions by **running a simulation of the future** and evaluating the outcomes of potential actions. By storing causal and structural relationships, it lets people take *new actions in new situations* and adapt behavior to immediate needs — deciding between job offers, creatively solving an unfamiliar problem.

How it works and its trade-offs:

- It stores information as **maps** — long known for physical environments (place/grid cells), now understood to also map cause-and-effect and abstract structural relationships. Deliberation is thus a **navigational** system: it navigates physical spaces and abstract idea-spaces alike.
- It is **flexible and creative** but **computationally and energetically expensive** — "deliberation is physically tiring." It can run slowly (complex situations) or quickly (simple ones), often uses heuristics, but **cannot run multiple simultaneous simulations**. It therefore cannot, by itself, manage the load of complex multi-subskill performance.
- It learns by **mentally navigating and exploring** the outcomes of possible choices and the structure of interrelated ideas.

**Teaching implications.** Learners train this system when they must think strategically, especially when a sequence of exercises yields *unique outcomes for different strategies*. Rich deliberative maps require **variation** — students who follow the same reasoning and take the same action every time never explore the knowledge structure. Exploration usually takes the form of time-consuming discussion of dead ends, inefficient paths, and unsatisfying solutions; this is in **direct tension with procedural repetition**, so deliberative and procedural exercises are best kept separate. The corresponding feedback is **"why"** — the causal consequences of an action in a situation. A canonical technique: give a hypothetical scenario, have the learner choose (e.g. a diagnosis/treatment), then repeatedly alter the scenario slightly so they must decide whether to change their choice — building causal reasoning that transfers to novel situations. Contrasting cases should contrast *outcomes from a given choice*.
